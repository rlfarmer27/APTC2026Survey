Sample Overview

Welcome to the 2026 APTC Annual Survey results. This presentation covers key findings from our membership survey, including sample demographics, salary data, clinic operations, and director wellbeing.

Who Responded?

Sample Highlights

  • N = 120 total respondents
  • 111 full members (92.5%)
  • 6 associate members

Note

Throughout this presentation, n’s vary because not everyone answered every question.

Our sample includes 120 respondents, with 92% being full APTC members. Sample sizes vary across analyses due to item-level missingness.

Professional Background

The vast majority of clinic directors hold doctoral degrees in psychology, with Ph.D. being the most common credential.

Degree Area

Most clinic directors specialize in clinical psychology, though counseling and school psychology are also well-represented.

Position Characteristics

Academic Rank

Associate and Assistant Professor ranks are most common among clinic directors. A notable proportion hold staff positions without faculty rank.

The majority of clinic directors, across all ranks, hold non-tenure-track positions. Tenure-track positions are relatively rare for this role.

Who Do We Report To?

Most clinic directors report to a department chair or program director. The reporting structure reflects the academic nature of most training clinics.

Contract Characteristics

Contract lengths vary considerably, with yearly contracts and tenured/permanent positions being most common.

Contract Type (Months)

The vast majority of clinic directors hold 12-month contracts, which reflects the administrative and service demands of the role that extend beyond the traditional academic year.

Overall Salary Distribution

The median gross salary is approximately $104,000, with a mean of $106,000. There is considerable variation across positions.

Salary Funding Sources

Department and university funds are by far the most common salary source. Only a minority receive salary funding from clinic fees or grants, highlighting the academic rather than fee-for-service structure of most training clinics.

Clinic Operations

Clinic Age & Training Model

Most clinics have been operating for decades. The scientist-practitioner model remains the most common training approach.

Clinic Size: Clients & Trainees

Clinic sizes vary considerably. The median clinic serves around 110 clients and trains approximately 15 students per year.

Clinic Space

Most clinics fall in the 1,000 to 5,000 square foot range, with the most common category being 2,000-4,999 square feet.

Staff Composition

About half of clinics report having support staff (57%) or graduate staff (50%). Fewer have undergraduate staff (29%), and postdoctoral trainees remain rare (7%).

Director Responsibilities

Administration and supervision are near-universal responsibilities. Direct patient services, teaching, and research are also common, underscoring the multi-faceted nature of the clinic director role.

Services & Fees

Services Offered

Service Highlights

  • 97% offer therapy
  • 92% offer assessment
  • Most clinics offer both services

Tip

89% of clinics use sliding scale fees

Nearly all clinics offer therapy services, with assessment also being very common. The majority use sliding scale fee structures.

Clinic Revenue

Median clinic revenue is approximately $44,000, though there is substantial variation. Some clinics generate over $500,000 annually.

Insurance Billing

Very few training clinics bill insurance, keeping administrative overhead low while maintaining accessible services through sliding scale fees.

Technology

EMR & Telehealth Adoption

Most clinics have adopted electronic medical records and offer telehealth services, reflecting the ongoing digital transformation in healthcare training.

Artificial Intelligence in Clinics

AI adoption among clinic directors remains limited—only about 13% report using AI in their responsibilities. Student AI use policies are still evolving, with many clinics having no formal policy in place.

AI Policy Status

Important

Most clinics do not yet have a formal AI policy. Many noted this is an upcoming priority.

The absence of formal AI policies mirrors broader trends in healthcare and academia. Clinics are navigating this space carefully, balancing innovation with ethical training considerations.

Routine Outcome Monitoring (ROM)

PHQ-9/GAD-7 and OQ-45 are among the most commonly used outcome monitoring tools. Routine outcome monitoring supports both training quality and client care.

Social Justice & DEI

Social Justice & DEI Efforts

DEI-focused didactics and antiracism resources for students are the most commonly reported activities. These data track ongoing commitment to social justice across APTC member clinics.

Research Activity

Note

Major research themes include clinical outcomes, measurement-based care, telehealth, and training evaluation.

About half of directors report being research-active in their clinics. Common themes include clinical outcomes research, ROM/measurement-based care, specialized populations, and implementation science.

Director Wellbeing

Burnout Indicators

Burnout scores vary across dimensions. These indicators help us understand the challenges clinic directors face and can inform support initiatives.

Burnout by Position Type

Comparing burnout across position types can reveal whether institutional security affects wellbeing. Higher scores indicate greater burnout.

What Helps & What Challenges Us

The Good ✓

  • Mentoring & training students
  • Supportive colleagues & department
  • Making a meaningful impact
  • Autonomy & flexibility in the role
  • Variety & engagement in work

The Challenging ✗

  • High workload & administrative burden
  • Lack of institutional support
  • Challenges with students & supervisees
  • Bureaucracy & institutional barriers
  • Pay disparities & lack of recognition

Note

These themes reflect open-ended survey responses and are consistent with patterns seen in prior years.

The same aspects of the job that directors find positive can also contribute to burnout when poorly supported—particularly supervision and student relationships. Colleagues and institutional support make a substantial difference.

What Helps Us Overcome Burnout?

  • Professional lifelines — APTC colleagues, peer consultation, mentorship
  • Saying “No” — strategic refusal, digital detox, protected time
  • Flexibility & autonomy — remote work, environment shifts, self-set hours
  • Self-care — personal therapy, external identities, humor & hobbies
  • Practical realities — salary, benefits, retirement vesting
  • Systemic problem solving — workable systems, delegating, advocacy

The 2026 survey moved to a 5-item CBI composite for greater reliability. Single-item comparison to prior year available via Burnout_1. BurnoutOvercome open-ended themes summarized in bullets.

Overcoming Burnout: Major Themes (1/2)

1. Professional Lifelines & Community

APTC colleagues, the listserv, and peer consultation serve as primary buffers. Leaning into the supervisor role is a frequent reset.

2. Reclaiming Boundaries & Saying “No”

Strategic refusal of extra roles, removing email from phones, protecting summers, limiting clinic hours.

3. Flexibility as a Mental Health Tool

Autonomy over hours and remote work are major reasons for staying. Many gain perspective vs. prior high-volume environments.

4. Self-Care Beyond the Surface

Personal therapy, decoupling identity from job title, investing in hobbies and humor outside psychology.

Overcoming Burnout: Major Themes (2/2)

5. Practical & Financial Realities

Salary, benefits, and retirement vesting are honest stabilizing factors. A few note lack of viable alternatives keeps them in the role.

6. Systemic Problem Solving

Building workable systems, delegating to administrative staff, refusing new initiatives until core needs are met.

What do you want to see more of?

  • Assessment practices and report writing styles

What do you want to see less of?

  • Space/square footage (“Girl, no. Do most people know the square footage of their clinic?”)
  • Detailed clinic operations — too varied to be useful across clinics
  • Going back to calculate prior year numbers under time pressure

A Case for Biennial Surveys

Note

Key clinic metrics show minimal year-to-year change — consistent with sampling variability rather than true shifts. Member feedback echoes this. A full survey every 2–3 years with a short annual pulse on fast-moving topics (AI, DEI, burnout) may better respect member time while preserving data quality.
